## Situation

On a 6×6 map with seize objective at (5,5), Blue's archer began at (5,0) and had a direct 3-turn path to victory. Red had symmetric force (archer + knight) but no inherent disadvantage. The map was small enough that positioning for combat seemed viable—yet neither side engaged in combat.

Blue's archer executed a methodical vertical push: (5,0) → (5,4) → (5,5), seizing the objective on turn 3. By turn 2, the archer was in attack range of Red's knight at (2,4), making it vulnerable. Red's units, meanwhile, positioned defensively toward the map center without committing to either:
- Blocking the seize point
- Aggressively eliminating the approaching archer
- Racing for the objective themselves

## Lesson

When the win condition is territorial (seize), unit commitment to a clear objective path beats reactive positioning. Blue succeeded not through superior positioning or combat but through singular focus: one unit, one route, one mission—execute without hesitation.

Red's distributed response—archer moving toward the center, knight moving toward the center—treated the scenario as a general skirmish rather than a race. This divided attention is the inverse of winning strategy on seize maps. Red could have won by:
1. Positioning its own archer at (5,5) before turn 3, or
2. Eliminating Blue's archer before it reached the objective

Instead, Red's delay in commitment meant tactical loss despite balanced forces.

**Generalizable principle:** On objective-based maps, identify the shortest path to the win condition and commit a unit entirely to it. Force the opponent to either counter that unit decisively or lose. Defensive positioning without an alternate win condition is a losing posture.
