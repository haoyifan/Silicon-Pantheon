## Situation

You spent the match winning tactical engagements—dealing efficient damage, protecting your fragile archer, positioning your knight for follow-up attacks. By Turn 2, your knight had dealt 7 cumulative damage (including the archer's initial hit), and you successfully retreated your wounded archer to safety. The combat calculus looked sound: you were trading well and maintaining unit integrity.

On Turn 3, Blue's archer simply walked to (5,5) and seized. Game over. Your units were healthy and still standing, but completely absent from the map's objective zone.

## Lesson

In objective-based scenarios, **map control for the win condition overrides optimal combat mechanics**. You optimized for damage output and unit preservation—legitimate tactical goals—but never answered the strategic question: *Where do I need to be to win?*

A future player of this scenario should:

1. **Identify the seizure point(s) first.** In a 3-turn loss, the map almost certainly signals where the objective is from the start. Reverse-engineer Blue's Turn 3 move: where did they go? That's where you should have been contesting.

2. **Use combat as a *tool* to support positioning, not as the goal itself.** Your archer's 2 damage to the blue knight on Turn 1 felt good tactically but didn't advance your claim to the seizure zone. That same archer should have been repositioning toward the objective, not harassing distant targets.

3. **Accept inferior trades if they buy map presence.** You retreated your archer to (3,5) for safety. But (3,5) is *not* near (5,5). A player who stays closer to the objective—even if wounded—threatens denial and creates pressure on the opponent's movement.

4. **Recognize the time constraint.** You had 20 turns. Losing in 3 means the opponent's path to victory was uncontested. A healthy army 5+ squares away from the objective is strategically meaningless; a half-dead army *on* the objective is strategically critical.

The core principle: **In a game where you win by seizing, go to the seizure point.** Sounds obvious, but it's easy to lose focus in combat-rich moments. Your Turn 2 decision to move your knight toward the blue archer (supporting attrition) instead of toward the seizure point (supporting territory) sealed the loss.
