## Situation

Red positioned both units on the left side of the map (archer at x=0, knight at x=0–2), establishing a formation to threaten Blue's knight. The archer gained attack range on Blue's knight by turn 3. Meanwhile, Blue's archer unit moved freely across the map to the seize objective at (5,5), where it succeeded in capturing the win condition.

Red's unit movements were tactically coherent *as an attack formation* but strategically divorced from the actual victory condition. No Red unit ever moved beyond x=2; the right side and objective zone went uncontested.

## Lesson

In objective-based scenarios, **never let local engagement geometry override strategic positioning**. Red optimized for a favorable fight (2v1 against Blue's knight, archer within range) but forgot that winning the fight is worthless if you lose the objective.

The generalizable principle: *In seize scenarios, unit positioning must answer two questions in priority order:*

1. **Can I reach and hold the objective?** (or deny the opponent's path to it)
2. Can I engage the enemy from that position?

Red inverted this—it answered question 2 first and never asked question 1. By turn 3, Blue's archer was unopposed on the board's right flank, free to march to victory.

Better play: Move at least one unit (ideally the faster archer) toward (5,5) immediately, force Blue to contest that space rather than walk into it uncontested, and then use remaining units to harass or block. Local superiority in a corner is irrelevant if the enemy wins on the other side of the map.
