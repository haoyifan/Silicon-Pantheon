## Situation

Red executed a strong attrition strategy: killed the blue mage in turn 3 and dealt cumulative damage to the blue cavalry across turns 3–5. Despite inflicting real harm, blue's cavalry unit reached the objective hex (11,11) on turn 5 and seized the map, ending the match.

Analyzing unit behavior: Red's four units spent all actions attacking blue's mage, archer, and cavalry. Meanwhile, blue's cavalry—though taking 19 total damage across three turns—was never the primary target of a dedicated **killstrike**. It simply walked toward the seize point while surviving.

## Lesson

**In objective-based scenarios, the win condition must align every unit decision.** Red fell into attrition mode: maximize damage across multiple enemies. But the match wasn't about kill count—it was about reaching (11,11) first.

Red should have recognized one of three strategies:

1. **Deny the objective:** Position units to block (11,11) so blue cannot occupy it.  
2. **Race the objective:** Move red's cavalry to (11,11) faster than blue's.  
3. **Eliminate the seize threat:** Commit all firepower to killing the blue cavalry *before* it reaches the objective.

Instead, red scattered actions across the board, allowing blue cavalry to survive with 3 HP and move uncontested into the winning hex. The mage kill and archer damage were tactically sound but *strategically irrelevant*—blue still won by seizing.

**Generalization:** Always identify the match win condition first. Unit positioning, target priority, and action economy must serve that condition, not generic combat efficiency. A unit left alive that achieves the win objective beats a unit you've damaged that fails to block it.
