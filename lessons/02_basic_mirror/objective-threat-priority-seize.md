## Situation

In the early-to-mid game, Red executed excellent tactical decisions: eliminated the enemy mage and archer through coordinated attacks, and began systematically damaging blue knight 1 (reducing it from 30 HP to 3 HP by turn 13). Red's knights remained at nearly full health (27 HP each), giving the appearance of a winning position.

However, turns 10–13 revealed a critical strategic gap. While Red focused entirely on grinding down blue knight 1, **blue knight 2 advanced completely unopposed down the eastern flank of the map**, moving from position (11,5) → (11,7) → (11,10) → (11,11)—directly into Red's fort. On turn 13, blue seized the objective and won.

## Lesson

**In objective-based scenarios (especially seize conditions), positional threats to your objective always outrank attrition damage to non-threatening units.**

Red treated the match as pure attrition: identify the weakest enemy and grind it down. But with a seize victory condition active, an uncontested enemy unit advancing on your fort is a *higher priority threat* than a wounded unit in the field—even if the wounded unit has higher remaining HP.

The corrective principle: **Assign at least one unit to deny or intercept any enemy with a clear approach vector to your objective.** This isn't about winning the fight; it's about controlling terrain. Red should have repositioned knight 2 (which spent turns 10–12 attacking blue knight 1) to instead block or threaten blue knight 2's advance corridor.

A 3 HP enemy knight is less dangerous than a 30 HP enemy knight at your doorstep. Positional threats to your win condition must be addressed *first*, even at the cost of delaying attrition elsewhere.
