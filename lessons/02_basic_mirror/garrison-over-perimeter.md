## Situation

In Turn 11, Red successfully positioned u_r_knight_2 directly on the fort (11,11)—the objective. This was sound: a unit occupying your own objective cannot be dislodged without being attacked and killed.

However, in Turn 12, Red moved that knight away to (10,11) to "physically block the direct path to the fort." This evacuation left the objective unoccupied. On Turn 13, Blue's knight moved from (10,10) to (11,11)—one diagonal step—and seized an empty fort.

## Lesson

When the objective is under immediate threat (within 1–2 moves), garrisoning it with one of your units is tactically superior to establishing a perimeter defense around it.

**Why:** A unit sitting on the objective forces an attacker to either (1) kill that unit first in place, or (2) find a separate path—both of which cost time and resources. An empty objective can be seized in a single move from an adjacent square, bypassing all your positional setup.

**When to apply:** If enemy units are closing in and you don't have the force to defeat them decisively, occupying your own fort with at least one defender creates an absolute barrier. Moving units away to form a perimeter assumes you can stop all approach routes—a risky assumption in a small arena where enemies can adjust their path.

**Trade-off:** A garrisoned unit may deal less damage or achieve worse trades, but preventing an instant loss is worth the opportunity cost. Winning individual skirmishes is irrelevant if you lose the objective to a simple move.
