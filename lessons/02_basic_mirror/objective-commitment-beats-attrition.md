## Situation

On a symmetric mirror map with a 20-turn max, Blue faced Red's aggressive early engagement—cavalry and archer pressing forward, dealing 4–7 damage to Blue units by turn 2. Red committed to forward positioning and damage output, clustering units around the center-right and lower-left zones. Blue's cavalry had the same 6-movement stat as Red's but was free to move.

The seize objective sat at (11, 11)—the far bottom-right corner, equidistant from both spawn sides.

## Lesson

**Single-unit objective races can overcome distributed attrition pressure.** While Red focused firepower on eliminating or damaging Blue's scattered units, Blue's cavalry executed a dedicated, uncontested sprint. By turn 2, the cavalry was already at (11, 5); by turn 3, (9, 9); by turn 4, it seized at (11, 11).

Red's units were too committed to the engagement zone to intercept or defend the objective in time. The cavalry traveled ~12 squares of map distance in 4 turns—unstopped—while Red's cavalry alternated between attacking (ending moves in suboptimal positions) and repositioning.

**Core principle:** In map-objective scenarios, commitment to a single win condition with high-mobility unit can outvalue distributed board control. Opponent attrition only matters if it prevents that objective push; if the objective can be reached before threats converge, board state becomes irrelevant. 

The win wasn't won by Blue's knights or archer surviving longer—it was won because Blue identified that Red's turn-1 aggression left a vulnerability: the objective was unguarded, and the cavalry was faster to it than Red could respond.
