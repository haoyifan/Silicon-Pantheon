## Situation

Tang Monk (blue VIP) occupied position (3,4) on turn 2, surrounded by allied units within 2–3 squares: Sun Wukong, Bai Long Ma, Sha Wujing, Zhu Bajie. On turn 3, Tang Monk moved west to (2,4), extending the distance to all supporting units. Red's White Bone Demon, positioned at (7,4) after turn 2, closed to (5,4) on turn 3—just one move away from the isolated VIP. By turn 4, White Bone Demon moved to (5,4) and killed Tang Monk in a single, uncontested attack.

## Lesson

**Objective units need sustained protective geometry, not defensive independence.** Blue spent actions on distributed offense (killing enemy units across the map) while the VIP gradually separated from its guard formation. The loss was not caused by failing to kill enemy units first or by bad positioning in a vacuum—it was caused by **moving the objective unit away from the force designed to protect it**.

Generalizable principle: In games where one unit must survive (VIP, escort, flagship), the protective formation must be *maintained actively*. This means:

- **Protect through proximity, not reaction.** If your VIP is 3+ squares from nearest allies, you've already lost positional advantage. Enemies can reach and isolate it faster than you can converge.
- **Formation moves together.** When the VIP moves, the guard moves with it, not independently. Spreading units across the map for offense divorces them from their primary job.
- **Perimeter integrity matters more than extended offense.** One kill or two kills on enemy units means nothing if the VIP dies uncontested.

This principle applies broadly: in any asymmetric game (where one asset is irreplaceable), you sacrifice map control and distributed aggression for **consolidated defensive density** around that asset.
