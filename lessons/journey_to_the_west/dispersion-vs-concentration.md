## Situation

Red deployed units across a wide spatial distribution: Skeletons spread to y=2, y=8, and various x positions; Spider Spirits positioned at map edges (7,2) and (7,6); Bull Demon attacking alone at (7,4). Blue responded by consolidating all five units into a tight cluster around (4–6, 4–5), creating a mutual-support formation. Over the next three turns, Blue systematically eliminated Red's units one by one—no Red unit received meaningful backup, while every Blue unit had 2–4 allies within striking distance.

## Lesson

Dispersion without superiority is a losing strategy. Red had 7 units to Blue's 5, yet lost decisively because spatial separation forced unfavorable engagement math. Each Red unit fought isolated (1v2 or 1v1), while Blue fought consolidated (3v1 or 4v1 when attacking, 5v1 defending). The Bull Demon's opening attack on Sun Wukong exemplifies this: 3 damage dealt vs. 16 counter damage taken—a catastrophic trade that set the tone.

The tactical principle: **Concentration of force defeats dispersed strength because it creates local numerical advantage and synergistic defense.** In small-squad tactics, board control matters far less than density. A 5-unit blob can eliminate a 7-unit swarm by controlling the engagement point. Red should have either (1) consolidated earlier to contest Blue's cluster, (2) attacked with multiple units simultaneously to overwhelm a single target, or (3) used dispersion strategically (bait + pincer) rather than passively.

When the opponent consolidates, matching their formation density is critical—or attacking them before they form. Fighting dispersed against concentrated is a path to elimination.
